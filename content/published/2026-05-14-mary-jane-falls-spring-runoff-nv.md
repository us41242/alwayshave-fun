If you live in Vegas and you've never hiked Mary Jane Falls in May, I don't know what to tell you. The math is simple: this is one of the only actual waterfalls in Southern Nevada, and it only actually waterfalls for a few weeks a year. We are in those weeks right now. The clock is ticking.

Today's score: **100/100**. Conditions: 50°F at the trailhead, AQI 32 ("Good," PM10 reading from AirNow), winds 3 mph with gusts to 5, zero chance of rain. The fire risk metric is low and the nearest active wildfire isn't even on the same mountain. Forecast through the weekend stays in the 60s with no precip. Riley and I are already planning the drive.

## Why May Is the Only Month This Trail Really Pays Off

Mary Jane Falls sits at the head of a small drainage on the east side of Mt Charleston, about 7,000 feet up in the Spring Mountains. The water you see falling off that limestone face isn't fed by a spring or a year-round creek — it's almost entirely snowmelt from the high country above. Mt Charleston caps out at 11,916 feet. The snowpack that accumulates there from December through March has exactly one place to go when it melts, and Mary Jane Falls is in the path.

What this means in practice:

- **March–early April:** Falls usually dry or barely seeping. Snow is still locked up. The road might also still be gated higher up if winter was strong.
- **Mid-April to late May:** Peak window. Snowmelt is in full swing. The falls actually look like a waterfall, not a wet stripe on rock. We are inside this window today.
- **June:** Volume drops fast. By the second week of June in a normal year, you can already see the falls thinning out.
- **July–October:** Dry. You can hike the same trail, get to the same alcove, and see basically wet streaks on the limestone. Pretty, but not a waterfall.

The whole reason to drive 45 minutes from the Strip to do this hike is the falls. If you go in August expecting what you see in the Instagram photos, you're going to be annoyed. May is the answer.

## What the Hike Actually Is

Mary Jane Falls is 5.2 miles round trip with 1,200 feet of elevation gain. The official rating is moderate. I'd call it moderate-and-honest — there's no scrambling or exposure, but the gain is sustained on the way up. You'll be working.

The trail starts at the upper Kyle Canyon parking area near the Cathedral Rock trailhead. First mile is gentle, climbing through aspen groves and Engelmann spruce. Then you hit a series of switchbacks that take you up through limestone outcrops to the base of a cirque. The falls are in the back of that cirque, coming down a 100-foot face into a shallow pool.

The last quarter mile flattens out and crosses scree before depositing you under the falls. There's a tiny alcove behind/beside the falling water that you can stand in if you want to feel the cold spray. In May, when the volume is actually meaningful, it's properly impressive. Riley always tries to drink directly out of the splash zone, which is fine — the water is cold enough that he gets bored of it after about thirty seconds.

The return trip is the same trail. Loose dirt and small gravel on the switchbacks means you want to step deliberately on the way down. I've slipped on this descent more than once when I was trying to look at the view instead of my feet.

## Parking and Logistics

The trailhead is at the top of Kyle Canyon Road, about 45 minutes from the Las Vegas Strip via NV-95 north and NV-157 west. Get to Charleston Town and keep going past it until the road dead-ends at the upper Kyle Canyon day-use area. The Mary Jane Falls trailhead is on the right as you reach the loop.

**Parking situation today:** The notes on this trail in my conditions feed read "Popular weekend trail — arrive before 8am." That is not a suggestion. By 9 AM on a nice Saturday in May, the upper lot is full and people are creating creative parking arrangements on the shoulder. Forest Service is starting to actively ticket on busy weekends. Get there at 7:30 and you'll find a real spot.

Restrooms are at the trailhead. Bring water — there is none on the trail.

**Vehicle requirements:** Any. Kyle Canyon Road is paved all the way to the lot. No high-clearance needed.

**Fees:** Spring Mountains NRA is a fee area on certain access roads but the Kyle Canyon day-use lot has been free in my experience for the trailheads. The campgrounds and group sites charge. Bring a few bucks just in case the fee policy has shifted; the Forest Service signs it clearly at the entrance.

## Riley's Take (Dogs Welcome)

Mary Jane Falls is one of the better dog hikes in the Spring Mountains. Leash required (Forest Service rule on the Spring Mountains NRA trails), but the surface is friendly — dirt and rock with very little broken sharp scree until the last bit. Riley's an Australian Shepherd, so he's built for this kind of grade and distance, but I've seen everything from corgis to Bernese mountain dogs on this trail without issue.

The water at the top is the main event for him. There's a pool he can drink from at the base of the falls (cold, clean snowmelt — it's about as good as creek water gets in Nevada). Bring a collapsible bowl anyway. He's also a big fan of the aspen sections in the lower mile, presumably because aspen groves smell like every interesting thing a dog could ever want to investigate.

One real warning: Aussies and herding breeds in particular can overdo it on cool days like this. The mild 50°F temperature lets them keep running when they'd otherwise self-regulate. Watch their pace, especially on the way up. Riley once tried to do this hike as a sprint and was useless for the next twenty-four hours.

## Gear You Actually Need

- **Real shoes.** Not running shoes. Trail runners minimum, low-cut hikers ideal. The switchback gravel will eat thin soles.
- **One liter of water per person.** Two if you're slow or it's warmer than today.
- **Light layer.** Even at 60°F, the cirque at the falls is shaded and noticeably cooler than the parking lot.
- **Sunscreen and a hat.** UV index 9 — the Mojave does not care that it's only 60 out.
- **Camera.** This is the actual reason you're going. Phone is fine but a real camera with a polarizer earns its keep on the limestone-and-water shots.
- **Leash for the dog.** Required, enforced, don't be that person.

What you do *not* need: poles (unless your knees are already complaining), microspikes (snow at this elevation should be gone by now), or any kind of permit. This is a walk-up trail.

## Best Time of Day to Go

Two windows are good:

- **Early — 7 AM to 9 AM start.** You get the parking, the cool air, the morning light on the limestone, and you're back at the car before the day-trippers from Vegas show up. This is what I'll be doing.
- **Late afternoon — 4 PM to 6 PM start.** Counterintuitive, but the falls face roughly east, so they're in shadow most of the afternoon. By 4 PM the crowds are gone, the temperature is dropping back into the comfortable zone, and you can be off the trail by sunset at 7:40. The downside is everything below the falls is in deeper shade too, so the photos are flatter.

The window you want to avoid is 11 AM to 2 PM on a weekend. The lot is overflowing, the trail is conga-lined, and the alcove at the falls is full of people having tripod meetings.

## A Quick Note on Whether This Is Actually a 100

The conditions score on alwayshave.fun is a weighted blend of temperature, AQI, wind, rain probability, fire proximity, and a few other variables. A 100 doesn't mean perfect for *every* hiker — it means the conditions themselves are pristine. For this specific trail in May with snowmelt running, the score is also accidentally capturing the seasonal sweet spot, which is the part the algorithm doesn't actually know.

What I mean is: in November, this same trail in identical 50°F-no-wind-AQI-32 conditions would also score 100. But the falls would be a wet stripe and you'd be staring at it going "okay, sure." May 100s on Mary Jane Falls and November 100s are not the same experience. Today is the kind. November is the other kind. The site can't tell you that — I can.
